Plague and History

There have been as many plagues as wars in history; yet always plagues and wars take people equally by surprise
Camus, The Plague (1947)

The advent of SARS-CoV-2 in late 2019 precipitated a major reorganization of contemporary social and economic relations, while also calling into questions a variety of individual and collective habits. For most of us in the West, the pandemic appears as an "unprecedented" event; and yet, it has many antecedents in recent and distant history. The Justinian Plague may well have brought down the East Roman Empire, while Smallpox devastated the indigenous peoples of the Americas. Cholera Pandemics raced across the Eurasian landmass in the early Nineteenth Century, and the Invluenza Pandemic of 1918-19 killed millions.

Can we understand our present and future by making sense of the past?
